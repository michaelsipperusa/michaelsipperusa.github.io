Understanding American Culture & Language

Explore the diverse landscape, cultures, and traditions of the United States

Welcome to American Culture Studies

The United States is a vast and prosperous nation, home to people from every corner of the world. Understanding American culture, language, and customs is essential for English language learners who want to navigate academic, professional, and social situations effectively. This page provides comprehensive resources about American geography, landmarks, regional cultures, ethnic communities, food traditions, and practical cultural knowledge.

Whether you're preparing to study in the U.S., planning to visit, or simply want to understand American English in its cultural context, these resources will help you appreciate the robust quality of American life. From the iconic landmarks of major cities to a plethora of immigrant communities that shape American identity, you'll discover what makes the United States unique.

All resources are sourced primarily from American English (U.S. Department of State) and other trusted educational sources to ensure accuracy and authenticity.

Major Regions of the United States

The Northeast

States: Maine, New Hampshire, Vermont, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, Connecticut, New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania

Cultural Characteristics: Historic colonial heritage, dense urban centers, academic institutions, diverse immigrant populations, four distinct seasons

Major Cities: New York City, Boston, Philadelphia, Washington D.C. (Mid-Atlantic)

Famous Landmarks:

  • Statue of Liberty (New York) - Symbol of freedom and immigration
  • Independence Hall (Philadelphia) - Birthplace of American democracy
  • Freedom Trail (Boston) - Historic revolutionary war sites
  • Niagara Falls (New York) - Natural wonder on Canadian border

Language Notes: Varied accents including distinctive New York, Boston, and Philadelphia dialects. Fast-paced speech patterns common in urban areas.

The South

States: Virginia, West Virginia, Kentucky, Tennessee, North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia, Florida, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas

Cultural Characteristics: Known for hospitality, slower pace of life, rich musical heritage (blues, jazz, country), complex history regarding civil rights, strong religious traditions

Major Cities: Atlanta, Miami, New Orleans, Nashville, Charlotte

Famous Landmarks:

  • French Quarter (New Orleans) - Historic district with unique Creole culture
  • Great Smoky Mountains - Most visited national park
  • Kennedy Space Center (Florida) - NASA's launch facility
  • Civil Rights Trail - Historic sites across multiple Southern states

Language Notes: Southern accent characterized by slower speech, distinctive vowel sounds, and unique vocabulary ("y'all," "fixing to," "bless your heart").

The Midwest

States: Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, Illinois, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Iowa, Missouri, North Dakota, South Dakota, Nebraska, Kansas

Cultural Characteristics: "Heartland" values, agricultural heritage, manufacturing centers, friendly and straightforward communication style, extreme weather (hot summers, cold winters)

Major Cities: Chicago, Detroit, Minneapolis, St. Louis, Kansas City

Famous Landmarks:

  • Mount Rushmore (South Dakota) - Presidential monument
  • Gateway Arch (St. Louis) - Symbol of westward expansion
  • Mall of America (Minnesota) - Largest shopping mall in U.S.
  • The Great Lakes - Largest group of freshwater lakes on Earth

Language Notes: Often considered "standard" American English. Clear pronunciation, friendly tone, distinctive expressions ("ope," "you betcha").

The West

States: Montana, Wyoming, Colorado, New Mexico, Idaho, Utah, Nevada, Arizona, Alaska, Hawaii

Cultural Characteristics: Outdoor lifestyle, environmental consciousness, Native American heritage, mining and ranching history, diverse landscapes from deserts to mountains

Major Cities: Denver, Phoenix, Las Vegas, Albuquerque, Salt Lake City, Anchorage, Honolulu

Famous Landmarks:

  • Grand Canyon (Arizona) - Natural wonder
  • Rocky Mountains - Major mountain range spanning multiple states
  • Yellowstone National Park (Wyoming/Montana/Idaho) - First national park
  • Las Vegas Strip (Nevada) - Entertainment capital
  • Pearl Harbor (Hawaii) - Historic WWII site
  • Denali (Alaska) - Highest peak in North America

Language Notes: Generally neutral accent, outdoor and environmental vocabulary common. Hawaii has unique pidgin English influenced by Polynesian and Asian languages.

The Pacific Coast (West Coast)

States: California, Oregon, Washington

Cultural Characteristics: Progressive values, tech industry, entertainment industry, diverse populations, environmental activism, laid-back lifestyle, Asian and Latino cultural influences

Major Cities: Los Angeles, San Francisco, San Diego, Seattle, Portland

Famous Landmarks:

  • Golden Gate Bridge (San Francisco) - Iconic suspension bridge
  • Hollywood Sign (Los Angeles) - Symbol of entertainment industry
  • Space Needle (Seattle) - Futuristic observation tower
  • Redwood National Park (California) - Ancient giant trees
  • Silicon Valley (California) - Technology innovation hub

Language Notes: California accent features distinct vowel shifts ("dude," "like," "hella" in Northern California). Tech jargon common in Bay Area.

ACADEMIC CULTURE

Understanding the American educational system and classroom expectations

Key Differences in American Academic Culture

American universities operate differently from educational systems in many other countries. Understanding these differences will help you succeed academically and avoid common misunderstandings.

Classroom Expectations

Class Participation

Expectation: Active participation is not just encouraged—it's often required and graded. Students are expected to speak up, ask questions, and contribute to discussions.

Why it matters: In American education, critical thinking and expressing your own ideas are highly valued. Silence can be interpreted as disengagement or lack of preparation.

Tips for Success:

  • Prepare questions before class
  • Don't wait to be called on—raise your hand
  • It's okay to disagree respectfully with professors or classmates
  • Build on others' comments: "I agree with Sarah's point, and I'd like to add..."

Working with Professors

Office Hours: Professors set aside specific times each week to meet with students. Attending office hours is expected and shows engagement, not weakness.

Communication Style: American professors typically prefer direct, informal communication. Most use first names or "Professor [Last Name]" rather than formal titles.

What to discuss in office hours:

  • Clarification on assignments or lectures
  • Feedback on drafts (if permitted)
  • Academic advising and career guidance
  • Research opportunities

Email etiquette: Use clear subject lines, professional greeting, concise message, and proper closing. Response times are usually 24-48 hours.

Academic Integrity

Critical importance: Plagiarism and cheating are taken extremely seriously in American universities and can result in failing grades, suspension, or expulsion.

What counts as plagiarism:

  • Copying text without citation
  • Paraphrasing without attribution
  • Submitting someone else's work as your own
  • Reusing your own previous work without permission ("self-plagiarism")
  • Having someone else write your paper

Resources:

  • Purdue OWL Citation Guide
  • American English Academic Writing Resources

Group Work and Collaboration

Expectation: Group projects are common and require equal contribution from all members.

Cultural note: Americans value individual accountability within group settings. Each person is expected to do their share.

Tips:

  • Establish clear roles and deadlines early
  • Communicate regularly via email or messaging apps
  • Address issues directly if someone isn't contributing
  • Document your individual contributions

Presentation Skills

American Presentation Style

Characteristics:

  • Direct eye contact with audience (shows confidence)
  • Clear structure: introduction, main points, conclusion
  • Visual aids (PowerPoint, Prezi) are standard
  • Interactive elements (questions to audience, discussions)
  • Professional but conversational tone

Common phrases:

  • "Today I'll be discussing..."
  • "Let's take a closer look at..."
  • "As you can see from this data..."
  • "To sum up / In conclusion..."
  • "Are there any questions?"
EVERYDAY LIFE & CUSTOMS

Navigate daily interactions and social situations in American culture

Social Customs and Etiquette

Greetings and Small Talk

Common greetings:

  • "Hi" / "Hello" / "Hey" (informal)
  • "How are you?" / "How's it going?" (not always expecting detailed answer)
  • "What's up?" (very informal, among friends)

Typical response: "Good, how are you?" or "Pretty good, thanks!" Even if you're not feeling well, brief positive responses are standard in casual interactions.

Small talk topics:

  • Weather (always safe!)
  • Weekend plans or recent activities
  • Sports, movies, TV shows
  • Work or school (general topics)

Topics to avoid with new acquaintances: Age, salary, politics, religion, weight, personal relationships

Personal Space and Physical Contact

Personal space: Americans typically maintain about 1.5-2 feet of distance in conversations. Standing too close can make people uncomfortable.

Physical contact:

  • Handshakes: Standard for business and formal introductions (firm grip, 2-3 shakes)
  • Hugs: Common among friends, but ask first if unsure
  • Cheek kissing: Rare except in some ethnic communities
  • Back patting: Sometimes used in congratulations or encouragement

Time and Punctuality

American time culture: Punctuality is very important and expected in most situations.

Guidelines:

  • Professional/Academic: Arrive 5-10 minutes early
  • Social gatherings: Arrive within 15 minutes of stated time
  • Dinner parties: Arriving exactly on time or 5-10 minutes late is acceptable
  • If running late: Always text or call to inform the person

Cultural note: "Time is money" is a common American saying reflecting the value placed on punctuality and efficiency.

Common American Expressions and Idioms

Everyday Expressions

Polite phrases:

  • "Excuse me" - To get attention or apologize for interrupting
  • "Pardon me" / "Sorry" - Apologizing for small mistakes
  • "No problem" / "No worries" - Casual response to thanks or apology
  • "You're welcome" / "My pleasure" - Response to "thank you"

Common idioms:

  • "Break a leg" - Good luck (especially for performances)
  • "Piece of cake" - Very easy
  • "Hit the books" - Start studying

ETHNIC COMMUNITIES & IMMIGRATION

The United States as a nation of immigrants

America's Immigrant Heritage

The United States has been shaped by successive waves of immigration from around the world. Today, over 14% of the U.S. population is foreign-born, and ethnic communities contribute significantly to American culture, economy, and society. Understanding these communities helps learners appreciate American diversity and connect with their own heritage while in the U.S.

Major Ethnic Communities

Hispanic and Latino Americans

Population: Approximately 62 million (19% of U.S. population)

Major origins: Mexico, Puerto Rico, Cuba, Dominican Republic, Central and South America

Concentration areas: California, Texas, Florida, New York, Arizona, Illinois

Cultural contributions:

  • Spanish language influence on American English
  • Cuisine: tacos, burritos, empanadas, Cuban sandwiches
  • Music: salsa, reggaeton, mariachi, Latin pop
  • Holidays: Cinco de Mayo, Día de los Muertos

Notable communities: East Los Angeles (CA), Miami (FL), San Antonio (TX), Washington Heights (NYC)

Asian Americans

Population: Approximately 24 million (7% of U.S. population)

Major origins: China, India, Philippines, Vietnam, Korea, Japan

Concentration areas: California, New York, Texas, New Jersey, Illinois, Washington

Cultural contributions:

  • Cuisine: Chinese, Japanese, Indian, Vietnamese, Korean restaurants ubiquitous in cities
  • Business and technology leadership (Silicon Valley)
  • Academic excellence and educational values
  • Martial arts, yoga, meditation practices

Notable communities: Chinatowns (San Francisco, NYC, Los Angeles), Little India (Edison, NJ), Koreatown (Los Angeles)

African Americans

Population: Approximately 47 million (14% of U.S. population)

History: Most African Americans are descendants of enslaved Africans brought to the U.S.; community has profoundly shaped American culture

Concentration areas: Southern states, major urban centers nationwide

Cultural contributions:

  • Music: jazz, blues, hip-hop, R&B, gospel
  • Literature and arts (Harlem Renaissance, contemporary writers)
  • Civil rights movement and social justice leadership
  • Cuisine: soul food, barbecue traditions, Southern cooking
  • Sports excellence and athletic culture

Important holidays: Juneteenth (June 19), Martin Luther King Jr. Day, Black History Month (February)

Middle Eastern and North African Americans

Population: Approximately 3.7 million

Major origins: Lebanon, Egypt, Syria, Iran, Iraq, Morocco, Palestine

Concentration areas: Michigan (especially Dearborn), California, New York, New Jersey, Texas

Cultural contributions:

  • Cuisine: falafel, shawarma, hummus, kebabs now mainstream
  • Business entrepreneurship (especially restaurants and retail)
  • Medical and engineering professions
  • Arabic language and Islamic cultural influence

Eastern European Americans

Major origins: Poland, Russia, Ukraine, Romania, Czech Republic, Hungary

Historical waves: Late 19th/early 20th century (industrial workers), post-WWII refugees, post-Soviet Union immigration

Concentration areas: Northeast cities, Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland

Cultural contributions:

  • Labor movement and union organizing
  • Catholic and Orthodox Christian traditions
  • Cuisine: pierogies, kielbasa, borscht, stuffed cabbage
  • Strong emphasis on education and family
TURKISH-AMERICAN COMMUNITY

Understanding the Turkish presence and cultural contributions in the United States

Turkish Americans: A Growing Community

Turkish Americans represent a vibrant and growing community in the United States, with an estimated 350,000-500,000 people of Turkish origin. While smaller than some other ethnic communities, Turkish Americans have made significant contributions to American society in business, science, medicine, arts, and culture.

Turkish-American Demographics and Settlement

Where Turkish Americans Live

Major concentration areas:

  • New York/New Jersey: Largest Turkish population, especially in:
    • Paterson, New Jersey (significant Turkish community)
    • Brooklyn and Queens, New York
    • Northern New Jersey suburbs
  • California: Los Angeles, San Francisco Bay Area, San Diego
  • Texas: Houston, Dallas, Austin
  • Florida: Miami, Orlando, Tampa
  • Illinois: Chicago metropolitan area
  • Virginia/Maryland/DC: Washington DC metro area
  • Michigan: Detroit area

Immigration Waves

Historical periods:

  • 1820s-1920s: Small numbers from Ottoman Empire (often counted as "Turkish" but diverse ethnic backgrounds)
  • 1950s-1970s: Students and professionals seeking education and career opportunities
  • 1980s-2000s: Economic migrants, entrepreneurs, and professionals
  • 2000s-present: Diverse immigration including students, tech workers, business investors, and political refugees

Turkish Cultural Presence in America

Turkish Cuisine in America

Popular Turkish restaurants and foods:

  • Döner: Turkish-style rotisserie meat now common in major cities
  • Turkish kebabs: Adana, şiş kebab, köfte widely available
  • Breakfast items: Börek, simit, menemen gaining popularity
  • Desserts: Baklava, Turkish delight, künefe in specialty stores
  • Beverages: Turkish coffee, çay (tea), ayran

Notable Turkish restaurant areas:

  • Paterson, NJ - Concentration of Turkish restaurants and bakeries
  • Brooklyn, NY - Turkish cafes and kebab shops
  • Los Angeles - Turkish fine dining and casual eateries
  • Major cities nationwide - Growing presence of Turkish cuisine

Turkish Cultural Organizations

Community organizations:

  • Turkish American associations: Nearly every major city has a Turkish-American cultural organization offering:
    • Language classes (Turkish for children of immigrants)
    • Cultural events and festivals
    • Holiday celebrations (Republic Day - October 29, Independence Day - August 30)
    • Community support and networking
  • Student organizations: Turkish student associations at major universities
  • Business networks: Turkish-American chambers of commerce
  • Cultural centers: Turkish cultural centers offering art, music, and educational programs

Turkish Contributions to American Society

Notable fields:

  • Medicine and Science: Turkish-American doctors, researchers, and scientists in major universities and research institutions
  • Technology: Engineers and entrepreneurs in Silicon Valley and tech hubs
  • Business: Small business owners (restaurants, retail, services) and corporate leaders
  • Academia: Professors and researchers at American universities, with Nobel Prize winners
  • Arts and Entertainment: Musicians, filmmakers, and artists

Notable Turkish Americans:

  • Dr. Mehmet Oz: Cardiothoracic surgeon, TV personality, former political candidate
  • Ahmet Ertegün: Founder of Atlantic Records (deceased 2006), music industry pioneer
  • Daron Acemoglu: Nobel Prize Winning Economist
  • Haluk Bilginer: Actor (though primarily works in UK/Turkey)

Turkish Language and Identity

Maintaining Turkish Heritage

Language preservation:

  • Turkish language schools in major cities (weekend programs for children)
  • Turkish media: TV channels (TRT World), newspapers, online content
  • Bilingual education emphasis in Turkish-American families

Religious institutions:

  • Turkish mosques and Islamic centers in major cities
  • Cultural events during Ramadan and Islamic holidays
  • Community gathering spaces beyond religious functions

Dual identity: Many Turkish Americans maintain strong connections to Turkey while fully participating in American society, representing a successful integration model.

Turkish-American Cultural Events

Annual Celebrations and Festivals

  • Turkish Festival (multiple cities): Annual outdoor festivals featuring Turkish food, music, dance, and crafts
  • Republic Day (October 29): Commemorating the founding of modern Turkey with community gatherings
  • National Sovereignty and Children's Day (April 23): Special celebrations for children
  • Cultural performances: Traditional Turkish folk dance (halay, zeybek), music concerts

Resources for Turkish and Turkic Language Speakers Learning English

Specific Challenges and Tips

Common difficulties for Turkish and Turkic language speakers:

  • Articles (a/an/the): Turkish doesn't use articles, so this requires extra practice
  • Word order: Turkish is SOV (Subject-Object-Verb), English is SVO (Subject-Verb-Object)
  • Phrasal verbs: These don't exist in Turkish and require memorization
  • Th sounds: /θ/ and /ð/ don't exist in Turkish pronunciation
  • Present perfect tense: Different usage than Turkish past tense forms

Advantages Turkish speakers have:

  • Many French-origin words in Turkish are also in English
  • Phonetic Turkish spelling helps with English pronunciation awareness
  • Turkish grammar complexity prepares learners for structured language study
AMERICAN FOOD CULTURE

Understanding American cuisine, dining customs, and food traditions

Classic American Foods

Traditional American Dishes

Iconic foods:

  • Hamburgers: Ground beef patty on a bun, America's most iconic food
  • Hot dogs: Sausage in a bun, especially popular at sporting events
  • Pizza: Italian-American creation, now definitively American
  • Fried chicken: Southern specialty, now national favorite
  • Barbecue: Regional varieties (Texas brisket, Carolina pulled pork, Kansas City ribs)
  • Mac and cheese: Comfort food classic
  • Apple pie: "As American as apple pie" - classic dessert
  • Thanksgiving turkey: Roasted turkey with stuffing, cranberry sauce, mashed potatoes

Regional Specialties

Northeast:

  • New England clam chowder (creamy seafood soup)
  • Lobster rolls (Maine)
  • Philly cheesesteak (Philadelphia)
  • New York-style pizza (thin crust, large slices)
  • Bagels with lox and cream cheese (New York)

South:

  • Fried chicken and waffles
  • Gumbo and jambalaya (Louisiana)
  • Shrimp and grits
  • Biscuits and gravy
  • Pecan pie
  • Key lime pie (Florida)

Midwest:

  • Chicago deep-dish pizza
  • Bratwurst (Wisconsin)
  • Hotdish/casseroles (Minnesota)
  • Cincinnati chili

West:

  • Tex-Mex food (tacos, fajitas, nachos)
  • California rolls (sushi)
  • Fish tacos (California/San Diego)
  • Green chile (New Mexico)
  • Sourdough bread (San Francisco)

American Dining Customs

Restaurant Etiquette

Tipping culture:

  • Standard tip: 15-20% of the pre-tax bill (18% is common)
  • Excellent service: 20-25%
  • Poor service: 10-15% (still expected)
  • Why tipping is important: Servers typically earn below minimum wage and depend on tips
  • Other tipping: Bartenders ($1-2 per drink), delivery drivers (15-20%), hairdressers (15-20%)

Dining process:

  1. Wait to be seated (unless it's fast-casual or counter service)
  2. Server introduces themselves by name
  3. Water and menus provided immediately
  4. Server takes drink orders, then food orders
  5. Server checks back: "How is everything?"
  6. Request check when finished: "Could we have the check, please?"
  7. Leave tip in cash or add to credit card payment

Portions: American restaurant portions are typically very large. It's completely normal to ask for a "to-go box" or "doggy bag" to take leftovers home.

Dining Styles

Types of restaurants:

  • Fine dining: Expensive, formal, reservations required, dress code
  • Casual dining: Mid-price, sit-down service (Applebee's, Olive Garden)
  • Fast casual: Order at counter, higher quality than fast food (Chipotle, Panera)
  • Fast food: Quick service, inexpensive (McDonald's, Burger King)
  • Food trucks: Mobile vendors, often specialized cuisine, trendy in cities
  • Diners: Classic American, breakfast all day, comfort food

American Meal Culture

Typical American Meals

Breakfast (7-9 AM):

  • Eggs (scrambled, fried, omelet)
  • Bacon or sausage
  • Toast, bagels, or English muffins
  • Pancakes or waffles (especially weekends)
  • Cereal with milk
  • Coffee is standard beverage

Lunch (12-1 PM):

  • Often quick and light (30-60 minutes)
  • Sandwiches or salads
  • Fast food or fast casual
  • Leftovers from dinner
  • "Lunch break" may be eaten at desk (office culture)

Dinner (6-8 PM):

  • Main meal of the day
  • Protein (meat, chicken, fish) + sides
  • Vegetables and starch (potatoes, rice, pasta)
  • Family dinners less common than historically but still valued

Snacking culture: Americans snack frequently between meals. Common snacks include chips, cookies, fruit, granola bars, nuts.

Food Vocabulary and Expressions

Common Food Terms

Ordering phrases:

  • "I'll have..." / "Can I get..."
  • "For here or to go?" (eat in restaurant or take out)
  • "What do you recommend?"
  • "Does this come with..." (asking about sides)
  • "Can I substitute..." (change an ingredient)
  • "Hold the..." (remove an ingredient, e.g., "hold the onions")
  • "On the side" (dressing or sauce served separately)

American vs. British food terms:

  • French fries (not chips)
  • Chips (not crisps)
  • Cookies (not biscuits)
  • Biscuits (similar to scones, not cookies)
  • Soda/pop (not fizzy drink)
  • Eggplant (not aubergine)
  • Zucchini (not courgette)
MEDIA & ENTERTAINMENT

Learn English through American movies, TV, music, and popular culture

Recommended TV Shows for English Learners

Beginner-Friendly Shows

  • Friends: Classic sitcom, clear speech, everyday situations (available on streaming)
  • The Office: Workplace comedy, mockumentary style, realistic dialogue
  • Modern Family: Family sitcom, multiple storylines, clear humor
  • Parks and Recreation: Government workplace comedy, optimistic tone
  • How I Met Your Mother: Friend group dynamics, romantic comedy

Why these work: Sitcoms have clear dialogue, repeated situations, and visual context that helps comprehension. Use subtitles initially!

News and Educational Programming

  • 60 Minutes (CBS): Long-running news magazine, in-depth reports
  • PBS NewsHour: Comprehensive daily news, slower-paced, clear language
  • Sunday Morning (CBS): Arts, culture, nature stories
  • Late night talk shows: Current events with humor (Stephen Colbert, Jimmy Fallon)
  • Documentary series: Planet Earth, How It's Made, American Experience

Tip: News programs provide exposure to formal English and current American issues.

American Music

Music Genres Born in America

  • Jazz: New Orleans origins, improvisation, swing, bebop
  • Blues: African American tradition, influenced rock and R&B
  • Rock and Roll: 1950s revolution, Elvis Presley, Chuck Berry
  • Country: Nashville sound, storytelling lyrics, rural themes
  • Hip-Hop/Rap: Bronx origins (1970s), now global phenomenon
  • R&B (Rhythm and Blues): Soul, Motown, contemporary R&B

For language learning: Country and folk music often have clear lyrics and storytelling; pop music exposes you to current slang and expressions.

American Sports Culture

Major American Sports

The "Big Four" Professional Sports:

  • American Football (NFL): Most popular sport, Super Bowl is major cultural event (February)
  • Basketball (NBA): Indoor sport, fast-paced, international appeal
  • Baseball (MLB): "America's pastime," slower pace, summer sport
  • Ice Hockey (NHL): Popular in northern states and Canada

College sports: College football and basketball have huge followings, especially in regions without professional teams.

Why sports matter for language learning:

  • Common conversation topic (especially Monday morning after weekend games)
  • Idioms from sports: "home run" (success), "Monday morning quarterback" (criticize after the fact), "drop the ball" (make a mistake)
  • Understanding American culture and community identity

  • NPR News: Current events, interviews, in-depth reporting

Transportation

Getting Around

Public transportation (varies by city):

  • Subway/Metro: Underground trains (NYC, DC, Boston, Chicago, SF)
  • Bus: Most cities have bus systems
  • Light rail/Streetcar: Some cities
  • Important note: Most American cities are car-dependent; public transit is limited outside major urban areas

Driving:

  • Driver's license from your state (requirements vary)
  • Cars drive on the RIGHT side of the road
  • Distances in miles (not kilometers)
  • "Gas station" = petrol station; gas/gasoline = petrol
  • Parking can be expensive in cities

Ride-sharing:

  • Uber and Lyft are dominant ride-sharing apps
  • Download app, request ride, pay through app
  • Tipping drivers 15-20% is customary

Shopping and Consumer Culture

Shopping Vocabulary

Store types:

  • Grocery store/Supermarket: Food and household items
  • Convenience store: Small store, higher prices, open late (7-Eleven)
  • Department store: Clothing, home goods (Macy's, Nordstrom)
  • Big box store: Large retailers (Walmart, Target, Costco)
  • Mall: Multiple stores under one roof

Important notes:

  • Sales tax: Prices on items DON'T include tax; tax added at checkout (varies by state, 0-10%)
  • Returns: Most stores allow returns within 30-90 days with receipt
  • Black Friday: Day after Thanksgiving, huge sales, shopping tradition

Customer service phrases:

  • "Can I help you find anything?"
  • "I'm just browsing, thanks" (looking, not ready to buy)
  • "Do you have this in a different size/color?"
  • "What's your return policy?"
